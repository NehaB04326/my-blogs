Understanding the Fundamentals: What Are CAT and GMAT?

Before we dive into comparisons, let's establish what these exams actually test.

CAT (Common Admission Test) is India's premier MBA entrance exam, conducted annually by the IIMs. It's your gateway to the top 20 IIMs and over 1,000 B-schools across India. In 2024, approximately 3.29 lakh candidates appeared for CAT, competing for roughly 13,000 seats across IIMs.

GMAT (Graduate Management Admission Test), on the other hand, is a globally recognized exam accepted by over 7,000 business schools worldwide, including ISB Hyderabad, SP Jain, and Great Lakes in India. Unlike CAT's annual schedule, you can take the GMAT multiple times throughout the year.

The fundamental difference? CAT opens doors primarily in India, while GMAT gives you global flexibility.

Exam Pattern Breakdown: What You'll Actually Face

CAT Exam Structure

CAT consists of three sections completed in 120 minutes:

  • Verbal Ability and Reading Comprehension (VARC): 24 questions in 40 minutes
  • Data Interpretation and Logical Reasoning (DILR): 20 questions in 40 minutes
  • Quantitative Ability (QA): 22 questions in 40 minutes

Total: 66 questions with sectional time limits, meaning you cannot move between sections. Each section contains both MCQs (with negative marking of -1 for wrong answers) and TITA (Type In The Answer) questions with no negative marking.

The difficulty level is adaptive at the section level—meaning the questions are pre-determined but challenging throughout.

GMAT Exam Structure (Focus Edition)

The GMAT Focus Edition, launched in 2023, streamlined the test to 2 hours 15 minutes:

  • Quantitative Reasoning: 21 questions in 45 minutes
  • Verbal Reasoning: 23 questions in 45 minutes
  • Data Insights: 20 questions in 45 minutes

Total: 64 questions with the flexibility to move between questions within each section. There's no negative marking, but the exam is computer-adaptive at the question level—each answer you give determines the difficulty of your next question.

Key Insight: One PrepGrind student, Rahul from Pune, scored 99.2 percentile in CAT but found GMAT's adaptive nature more stressful initially. "In CAT, I could skip tough questions and return to them. GMAT forces you to commit," he shared.

Difficulty Level: Which Exam is Tougher?

This question doesn't have a straightforward answer because difficulty is subjective and depends on your strengths.

CAT is considered tougher if:

  • You struggle with time management under pressure
  • Your calculation speed isn't exceptional (QA section is notoriously challenging)
  • You find lengthy Reading Comprehension passages difficult to process quickly
  • You're not comfortable with the uncertainty of a once-a-year opportunity

GMAT is considered tougher if:

  • You get anxious with adaptive testing (each question matters immediately)
  • You struggle with Data Sufficiency questions (unique to GMAT)
  • You prefer the flexibility of skipping and returning to questions
  • You find integrated reasoning under time pressure challenging

According to data we've analyzed from our coaching programs, students with strong fundamentals in quant typically find CAT's DILR section the biggest hurdle (average accuracy: 60-65%), while GMAT test-takers struggle most with maintaining consistency due to the adaptive format.

Career Goals: The Deal-Breaker Factor

This is where you need brutal honesty with yourself.

Choose CAT if:

You want to study at IIMs or top Indian B-schools like FMS Delhi, XLRI, or MDI Gurgaon. CAT scores are your only entry ticket to these institutions. If you're targeting roles in Indian consulting firms, banking, or plan to work in India post-MBA, the IIM brand carries unparalleled weight.

Real Example: Priya, a PrepGrind student from 2023, chose CAT specifically for IIM Ahmedabad's strong alumni network in the consulting space she wanted to enter. She's now at a Big 4 firm, and she says her IIM-A stamp opened doors immediately.

Choose GMAT if:

You're keeping international options open. GMAT is accepted at Harvard, Stanford, Wharton, INSEAD, and other global powerhouses. In India, ISB (both Hyderabad and Mohali campuses) primarily considers GMAT scores. If you're exploring one-year MBA programs or executive MBAs, GMAT is typically required.

Real Example: Arjun wanted flexibility. He prepared for GMAT, scored 715, and applied to both ISB Hyderabad and Duke Fuqua. He eventually chose ISB but had global backup options—something CAT alone wouldn't have provided.

The Hybrid Approach:

About 15% of our students prepare for both exams. They typically start with CAT (given its November timeline), then appear for GMAT in December-January if CAT doesn't go as planned. This requires 7-8 months of preparation but maximizes your chances.

Preparation Time and Resources

CAT Preparation Timeline:

Most successful candidates prepare for 6-9 months. The syllabus overlaps significantly with what you learned in school (Class 8-10 math), but the application is far more complex. You'll need to master:

  • 15-20 DILR set types
  • 8-10 Reading Comprehension varieties
  • 20+ Quantitative topics from Arithmetic to Modern Math

Resources are abundant: mock tests from IMS, TIME, Career Launcher, and PrepGrind's specialized practice modules.

GMAT Preparation Timeline:

GMAT typically requires 3-4 months of focused preparation, primarily because:

  • The syllabus is more defined and consistent
  • Official GMAT prep materials are excellent (GMAC provides actual test questions)
  • The adaptive nature means understanding question types matters more than solving 1000s of problems

Official resources like GMAT Official Guide and GMATPrep software are gold standards.

Cost Consideration:

CAT registration fee: ₹2,500 (approximately $30)
GMAT Focus Edition fee: $275 (approximately ₹23,000)

If you're taking GMAT multiple times (you can retake after 16 days), costs add up quickly.

Scoring and Percentiles: What's Considered Good?

CAT Scoring:

CAT uses a scaled score system with normalized percentiles. To get into top IIMs:

  • 99+ percentile for IIM A, B, C (typically 150+ scaled score out of 300)
  • 97-99 percentile for newer IIMs (140-150 scaled score)
  • 90-95 percentile for decent B-schools

Your sectional percentiles matter significantly. Even with a great overall score, if one section falls below the cutoff, you're out.

GMAT Scoring:

GMAT Focus Edition scores range from 205-805. For top schools:

  • 700+ (87th percentile) for ISB, top international programs
  • 650-700 (71-87 percentile) for good international MBAs
  • 600+ (56th percentile) for tier-2 programs

Unlike CAT, GMAT gives you detailed breakdowns—you can see exactly where you stand in each section.

The Flexibility Factor

CAT happens once a year (typically late November). Miss it or underperform, and you're waiting another year.

GMAT offers year-round testing. You can take it when you're ready, retake if needed (up to 5 times in 12 months), and strategically time your applications.

From a mental health perspective, this flexibility is massive. I've seen students completely freeze during CAT because they knew it was "now or next year." GMAT removes some of that pressure.

Making Your Final Decision: A Framework

Ask yourself these five questions:

  1. Where do I want to study? (India-focused = CAT; Global options = GMAT)
  2. What's my risk tolerance? (One shot vs. multiple attempts)
  3. What's my budget? (₹2,500 vs. ₹23,000+)
  4. What's my timeline? (Immediate B-school vs. can wait)
  5. What's my career goal? (India-based role vs. international aspirations)

If you answered India/Low/Limited/Immediate/India-based—CAT is your exam.

If you answered Global/High/Flexible/Can wait/International—GMAT makes more sense.

If you're genuinely split, consider the hybrid approach or start with CAT (it's cheaper and happens first in the calendar year).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take both CAT and GMAT in the same year?

Absolutely. Many students take CAT in November and GMAT in December-February as a backup strategy. Since the quantitative and reasoning sections overlap significantly, preparing for both simultaneously is feasible. However, you should start CAT preparation first (due to its earlier date) and add GMAT-specific sections like Data Sufficiency later.

Which exam is better for someone weak in mathematics?

Neither exam is "easy" for math-weak students, but GMAT might be slightly more manageable. CAT's Quantitative Ability section is notoriously tough with complex problem-solving, while GMAT quant, though challenging, is more concept-based. However, GMAT's Data Sufficiency questions require a different thinking approach. The key is addressing your math weakness through systematic preparation, regardless of which exam you choose.

Do IIMs accept GMAT scores?

As of 2025, IIM Ahmedabad, Bangalore, and Calcutta accept GMAT/GRE scores only for their executive MBA or one-year MBA programs, not their flagship two-year residential programs. Some newer IIMs have started accepting GMAT for specific programs, but CAT remains the primary route for IIM admissions. Always check the specific IIM's official website for current admission policies.

How many times can I retake CAT vs GMAT?

CAT can be taken only once per year (you cannot retake in the same year even if you're unhappy with your score). GMAT can be taken up to 5 times in a rolling 12-month period and 8 times lifetime, with a mandatory 16-day gap between attempts. This retake flexibility is one of GMAT's biggest advantages.

Is CAT preparation enough for GMAT or do I need separate prep?

CAT preparation covers about 60-70% of GMAT content, especially in quant and reasoning. However, GMAT has unique question types like Data Sufficiency and Sentence Correction that require dedicated practice. The GMAT's computer-adaptive format also demands different test-taking strategies. If you've prepared for CAT, you'll need 4-6 additional weeks to adapt specifically for GMAT's format and unique sections.
